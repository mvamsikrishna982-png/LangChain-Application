Rick slammed the portal gun shut with a flourish that scattered a few loose screws across the garage floor. Morty, still clutching the small, humming orb they'd pulled from the guts of a collapsed reality, stared at the device like it might bite.

“Okay, Morty, listen,” Rick said, voice low and urgent, eyes glittering with that manic focus that meant trouble and brilliance in equal measure. “This thing—this little orb—doesn’t just map realities. It… negotiates them. It finds the seams where two universes are willing to trade a favor. We’re gonna use it to barter for a timeline where your math grade doesn’t ruin your life.”

Morty swallowed. “Barter with realities? Rick, that sounds—like, illegal? Immoral? Dangerous?”

Rick snorted. “Morty, morality’s a local ordinance. We’re going to a market.”

They stepped through a portal that smelled faintly of ozone and burnt coffee. The world on the other side was a bazaar of impossible geometries: stalls stacked like Möbius strips, vendors hawking bottled sunsets, a vendor with three mouths selling “genuine” alternate-history newspapers. The orb pulsed in Morty’s hand, and a translucent ribbon of light unfurled from it, pointing them toward a stall draped in shimmering fabric.

The stall’s proprietor was a being that looked like a cross between a librarian and a clockwork octopus. It spoke in a chorus of clicks. “You seek a timeline favor. Terms?”

Rick grinned. “We’ll trade you a single, unremarkable memory. One that’s been edited out of our timeline already. You take it, you give us a week where Morty aces algebra.”

The octopus-librarian clicked, its eyes—tiny hourglasses—tilting. “Memories are currency. But beware: memories are not inert. They carry echoes.”

Morty’s stomach dropped. “Echoes? Like, ghosts?”

“Like consequences,” Rick corrected. “But we can pick something small. Something… replaceable.”

They negotiated. Rick, with his practiced sleight of hand, offered a memory of a failed experiment that had already been erased from their personal timeline—an experiment that, in the original sequence, had only resulted in a singed lab coat and a mildly annoyed neighbor. The octopus-librarian accepted, siphoned the memory into a crystalline vial, and in return pressed a tiny, humming ticket into Morty’s palm. “Seven days. Use them wisely.”

Back home, the world felt subtly different. Morty’s algebra teacher, Ms. Kline, smiled at him in a way that suggested she’d always believed in him. The test he’d been dreading felt like a puzzle he could solve. For a week, Morty floated on a buoyant current of competence. Rick watched him with a mixture of pride and something like regret.

On the seventh night, the orb pulsed again. The ticket dissolved into dust. Morty’s confidence didn’t vanish—if anything, it hardened into a new, stubborn core. But the world around them had shifted in ways they hadn’t bargained for. The neighbor whose lawn had been singed in the erased experiment now had a different grievance: a missing cat that had never existed before. A newsfeed Rick glanced at mentioned a small, inexplicable uptick in temporal anomalies in a district three cities over.

“Echoes,” Morty whispered.

Rick’s face went hard. “We traded a memory that was supposed to be gone. The market took it and—like any market—spent it. It’s not about the memory itself; it’s about the pattern it created. Someone out there now remembers a cat that never was. Someone else remembers a protest that never happened. The fabric’s got a few new threads.”

They followed the trail of anomalies to a suburban cul-de-sac where reality shimmered like heat above asphalt. At the center of the distortion stood a small, unassuming house with a mailbox that read: “Temporal Exchange — Local Branch.”

Inside, a receptionist with a smile too wide and eyes like polished coins handed them a brochure. “We facilitate trades,” she chirped. “Memories, probabilities, regrets. Everything has a price.”

Rick’s jaw tightened. “You’re laundering memories.”

“We’re curators of possibility,” she corrected. “And sometimes, possibilities leak.”

Morty’s orb vibrated. The ribbon of light pointed to a back room where a wall of glass held rows of bottled moments: first kisses, lost keys, the taste of a summer peach. Each bottle hummed with a life of its own. One bottle, labeled in a neat, bureaucratic hand, contained a small, flickering scene: a boy in a classroom, raising his hand. Morty’s breath hitched.

“You can’t just take things that were erased,” Morty said. “They were erased for a reason.”

The receptionist’s smile didn’t falter. “Erasure is a service. We repurpose what would otherwise be waste. But sometimes repurposed things become invasive.”

Rick’s eyes scanned the room. “You’re not just trading memories. You’re seeding narratives. You plant a memory in one place, and it grows into a story that other minds pick up. Stories spread. They mutate. That’s how you get a cat that never existed becoming a missing-cat movement.”

They demanded the return of the memory. The receptionist shrugged. “Returns are possible, but only if you can offer an equivalent: a memory of equal narrative weight.”

Rick thought fast. He could fabricate a memory, but the Exchange’s scales were calibrated to authenticity. Morty’s week of algebra success had real emotional weight. They needed something that carried the same narrative heft.

Rick reached into his own past and pulled out something he’d never admit to: a memory of a quiet night, years ago, when he’d sat on a rooftop and watched a meteor shower with someone he’d once cared about. It was a small, private thing, but it had depth. He offered it.

The Exchange accepted. The bottled memory slid across the counter and merged with the one they’d traded. The receptionist smiled, and the distortions began to unwind. The missing-cat protests dissolved into confusion. The neighbor’s grievance faded. The world stitched itself back together, but not perfectly; there were tiny seams where the fabric had been mended, visible if you looked closely.

Morty felt the algebra confidence recede, but not entirely. He kept a residue of competence, a faint echo of the week that had been. Rick, however, felt something else settle in him: a small, warm ache where the rooftop memory had been taken. It was a price he’d paid willingly, and yet it lingered like a phantom limb.

Outside, as they stepped back into the garage, Morty looked at Rick. “Did we do the right thing?”

Rick lit a cigarette—an affectation he’d picked up in a dozen timelines—and exhaled. “Right and wrong are luxuries for stable timelines, Morty. We did what we had to do to stop a market from turning people’s lives into commodities. But we also learned something: memories aren’t just data. They’re anchors. Pull too many, and the ship drifts.”

Morty nodded, thinking of the boy in the classroom, the cat that never was, the week he’d felt like he could do anything. “So what now?”

Rick pocketed the orb. “Now we make sure the Exchange can’t scale. We find their supply lines, their buyers. We make memory laundering unprofitable. And if we have to, we’ll open a competing branch—one that gives people back what they didn’t know they lost.”

Morty smiled, small and uncertain. “Can we—can we keep a little of the algebra? Like, just enough to pass the next test?”

Rick’s grin softened. “I’ll code you a cheat sheet, Morty. Ethical gray area, but effective.”

They walked back into the house, the garage door closing behind them with a soft thud. Outside, the night sky was ordinary, the stars indifferent. Inside, the hum of the orb was quiet, like a sleeping thing. Somewhere, in a glass bottle on a shelf in a back room, a rooftop night shimmered, waiting for the next curious hand.

And Rick, for once, didn’t try to fix everything. He just sat at the workbench, tinkering with the portal gun, and let the world be messy for a little while.

The next morning, the garage smelled like solder and burnt toast. Rick was already hunched over the workbench, a constellation of spare parts and half-finished schematics spread out like a madman’s map. Morty hovered by the doorway, clutching a crumpled algebra sheet and a pencil with a chewed eraser.

“You sure you want to do this, Morty?” Rick asked without looking up. His voice had that brittle edge it got when he was both excited and trying to hide worry. “We poke the Exchange, we poke a market. Markets bite back.”

Morty nodded. “Yeah. People shouldn’t be—selling memories. What if someone buys something that ruins them? Or what if they sell something they need? I don’t want more cats that never existed.”

Rick snorted. “You’re sentimental. Cute. Fine. We’ll be surgical. We’ll hit their supply chain, not their customers. Take out the middlemen, leave the buyers confused and the sellers sober.”

They started with reconnaissance. Rick’s portal gun hummed as he dialed a coordinate he’d scraped from the Exchange’s public ledger—an ugly little thing, a blockchain of recollections that the Exchange used to track provenance. The ledger was public because the Exchange liked the optics of transparency; it also meant it left breadcrumbs.

Their first stop was a warehouse on the outskirts of a city that had been built on top of a collapsed timeline. The building looked like a thrift store for lost things: crates stamped with dates that never happened, pallets of boxed “first kisses,” and rows of glass vials humming with the faint light of other people’s yesterdays. A pair of security drones—flat, pancake-shaped things with polite smiles—floated in the rafters.

Rick pulled a small device from his coat: a temporal dampener that could scramble the Exchange’s provenance scanners for a few minutes. He handed Morty a pair of goggles. “You’re on lookout. If anyone comes, you whistle like a dying goose.”

Morty did not like the sound of that, but he put the goggles on and followed Rick through a side door. Inside, the air tasted like nostalgia and ozone. Workers—humanoid, insectile, and a few that looked suspiciously like alternate versions of people Rick recognized—moved with the practiced efficiency of a factory line. They cataloged memories, weighed them, and packaged them into neat little boxes for shipment.

Rick moved like a shadow, slipping past scanners and disabling locks with a flick of his wrist. He found the supply ledger: a long list of collectors and harvesters, names and coordinates, and a pattern that made his eyes narrow. The Exchange wasn’t just buying stray memories; it had contracts with “harvesters” who specialized in extracting memories from people who were vulnerable—those in hospitals, shelters, or on the edges of society. The Exchange paid well, and the harvesters paid better.

“That’s the problem,” Rick muttered. “They’re monetizing desperation.”

Morty’s stomach turned. “So they’re… stealing from people who can’t afford to keep their memories?”

“Not stealing,” Rick said. “Buying. But the ethics are the same when the alternative is rent or a bottle of something. We cut the supply, we cut the market.”

They planted a few devices—small, elegant things that would scramble the harvesters’ payment channels and leak their contracts to regulators in a dozen timelines. Rick also slipped a worm into the Exchange’s ledger: a little program that would, over time, inflate the provenance cost of any memory harvested from vulnerable populations until it became unprofitable. It was a slow burn, surgical and bureaucratic—Rick’s favorite kind of violence.

They left before the drones noticed. Morty felt like he’d done something important, but also like he’d left a bruise on the world. “Will that fix everything?” he asked as they stepped back through the portal.

Rick lit a cigarette and watched the smoke curl. “No. Nothing fixes everything. But it makes a market less hungry. It makes a few people think twice before selling their childhood for a week’s rent.”

For a few days, the Exchange’s supply lines hiccuped. Harvesters found their accounts frozen, contracts flagged, and buyers balking at the sudden spike in provenance fees. The Exchange responded with PR—smiling spokesbeings on holo-ads, promises of “ethical curation,” and a new line of memory insurance. The market adapted. Markets always adapt.

Then the Exchange retaliated.

It started small: a smear campaign that painted Rick as a “memory pirate” who trafficked in stolen recollections. A few of Rick’s old associates—people who owed him favors or feared him—began to distance themselves. Then the Exchange released a curated leak: a montage of memories that suggested Rick had once sold a memory that led to a small, but tragic, event in a timeline where a child lost a parent. The montage was edited to maximize outrage.

Rick watched the smear with a slow, dangerous smile. “They’re playing dirty. Fine. We play dirtier.”

He called in favors. He found allies in unlikely places: a retired archivist who ran a black-market library of banned histories, a group of ex-harvesters who’d been burned by the Exchange’s contracts, and an AI that specialized in legal loopholes and loved to argue with corporate policy. Together, they formed a ragtag coalition—part activist, part hacker, part vigilante.

Their plan was audacious. They would open a competing branch: a cooperative where people could deposit memories voluntarily, with full consent and legal safeguards. The cooperative would pay fair prices, provide social services, and—most importantly—return memories to donors if they regretted the sale. It would be transparent, community-run, and deliberately unprofitable for the Exchange.

Morty was skeptical. “You really think people will trust us after what the Exchange did?”

Rick shrugged. “Trust is a function of transparency and utility. We’ll give them both. And we’ll make the Exchange look like the predatory corporation it is.”

They launched the cooperative in a converted community center. The first donors were cautious: a woman who sold a memory of a painful breakup to pay for her child’s medicine, a man who traded a memory of a failed business to escape debt, a teenager who sold a memory of a bully to stop the nightmares. The cooperative offered counseling, legal aid, and a small stipend. It also recorded provenance in a way that made it easy to reverse transactions.

Word spread. People who had been exploited by the Exchange found a safer alternative. The cooperative’s ledger—open-source and auditable—became a model for ethical memory commerce. The Exchange responded with lawsuits, smear pieces, and a few targeted raids. But the cooperative had something the Exchange didn’t: community support and a network of allies who were willing to fight.

One night, as Morty shelled peas in the cooperative’s kitchen, a man walked in with a box under his arm. He looked like he’d been through a lot: hollow eyes, hands that trembled, and a voice that kept catching on the edges of sentences.

“I heard you help people get their memories back,” he said. “Mine… I sold it. I didn’t know what it would do. I can’t remember my daughter’s face.”

Morty felt his chest tighten. He led the man to a counselor. Rick, who had been tinkering with a new prototype of a memory stabilizer, watched from the doorway. For the first time in a long while, he didn’t have a plan that involved blowing something up. He had a plan that involved paperwork, therapy, and a lot of patience.

They worked for weeks. The cooperative’s tech team—part engineers, part therapists—reconstructed the provenance trail and negotiated a return with the Exchange. It wasn’t easy. The Exchange demanded compensation, citing “processing fees” and “curation costs.” Rick argued, threatened, and finally offered a compromise: a public audit of the Exchange’s practices in exchange for the memory’s return.

The audit was brutal. It exposed contracts that targeted the vulnerable, algorithms that prioritized profit over consent, and a pattern of laundering memories through shell accounts. Public outrage followed. Regulators in several timelines opened investigations. The Exchange’s stock—if you could call a market in memories a stock—took a hit.

The man got his daughter’s face back. He wept when he saw her in a recovered clip—small, stubborn, and alive. Morty watched him and felt something like hope.

But the victory was partial. The Exchange didn’t collapse. It adapted. It moved operations to jurisdictions with laxer laws, rebranded, and continued to operate in the shadows. The cooperative, meanwhile, struggled with funding, burnout, and the moral weight of deciding which memories to accept and which to refuse.

One evening, as Morty and Rick sat on the cooperative’s roof watching a sky that had been patched and repatched a dozen times, Morty asked the question that had been gnawing at him.

“Do you ever regret it, Rick? Giving up that rooftop memory?”

Rick was quiet for a long time. He traced a circle on the rooftop with his finger, the cigarette between his lips forgotten.

“Sometimes,” he said finally. “Memories are anchors. They keep you from drifting. But they also weigh you down. I gave it up because it mattered to me, and because I thought it would help stop something worse. I don’t know if it was the right trade. But I know this: if you never give anything up, you never get anything back.”

Morty thought about the boy in the classroom, the missing cat, the man who found his daughter. He thought about algebra tests and the way confidence could be both fragile and contagious.

“Maybe,” he said slowly, “we can make a world where people don’t have to sell their memories to survive.”

Rick snorted, but there was a softness in it. “Maybe. Or maybe we just make a world where the predators have to work a little harder. Either way, it’s better than doing nothing.”

They sat in silence as the city below hummed—markets, memories, and all the messy, human things that made timelines worth saving. The cooperative’s ledger glowed faintly on a nearby terminal, a small beacon of transparency in a sea of obfuscation.

A notification pinged on Rick’s wrist. A new donor had arrived: a woman with a memory of a lullaby she’d forgotten. She wanted to deposit it, not sell it—she wanted it preserved for her grandchildren. The cooperative’s counselors smiled. The archivist took the vial with reverence.

Morty smiled too. It was a small thing, but it felt like progress.

Rick flicked his cigarette into the night and crushed it with his boot. “Alright, Morty. We keep doing this. We poke the market, we patch the leaks, and we make sure people have choices. And if the Exchange comes for us again, we’ll be ready.”

Morty nodded. “Yeah. And maybe—maybe we can teach people how to keep their memories safe, so they don’t have to sell them in the first place.”

Rick grinned, the old, dangerous spark returning. “Now you’re thinking like an organizer. Next thing you know, you’ll be running for office.”

Morty laughed, a small, genuine sound. The rooftop felt less like a place of exile and more like a vantage point. Below them, the cooperative’s lights blinked in the dark, a constellation of small, stubborn resistances.

They had not fixed everything. They had not even fixed most things. But they had started something that mattered: a place where memories could be treated with dignity, where people could choose, and where the market’s appetite could be tempered by community.

And somewhere, in a back room of the Exchange, a bottled rooftop night shimmered, waiting. But now, there were more hands reaching for the shelves—hands that wanted to protect, to preserve, and to give back. The market would adapt. So would they.

Rick stood, stretched, and looked at Morty with a rare, unguarded expression. “Come on, Morty. Let’s go make some paperwork revolutionary.”

Morty followed him down the stairs, feeling the weight of the world and the lightness of having done something about it. Outside, the stars were ordinary and indifferent, but the people below were not. They were messy, stubborn, and alive—and for now, that was enough.
